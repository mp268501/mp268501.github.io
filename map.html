Before I ever knew who Richard Feynman was, I understood this principle. A consulting project for Daimler Chrysler was a formative early work experience. I had a front row seat to see how a global company was fooling itself. The company had a legitimate strategy, but it was never going to execute it because Jurgen Schrempp and DCX's executives were living in a separate reality from the rest of the organization. I had a unique opportunity to interview people across the organization. The interviews painted a portrait of a dysfunctional company. My report mirrored reality back to Daimler but it didn't have an impact. A few years later, DCX imploded. I saw reality and it predicted the future.

I learned three things from my Daimler experience: 1. Interview to understand 2. Fooling themselves (it's pervasive, not always intentional, but its human nature) 3. Early work experiences as a kid--managers were wildly disconnected. Were they fooling themselves? I wasn't sure.
