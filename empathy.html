The Compounding Empathy Challenge

A Math Progression Simulation

The "Alien" Number System

To simulate a student's experience, you cannot rely on your intuition. All exercises use the system below. You must reason from the ground up.

Z = 0
A = 1
B = 2
C = 3
D = 4
E = 5

This is a Base-Six number system.

Exercise 1: Multiplicative vs. Additive Reasoning

Solve the following multiplication problem using the traditional algorithm. Use the letter-based system and show your work for the partial products.

DC
×AB

The Intended Frustration

You will feel a heavy cognitive load. Each step requires conscious, deliberate calculation and conversion between base-ten thinking (to figure out the product) and base-six representation (to write it down). You will likely make mistakes by accidentally reverting to base-ten rules (e.g., "carrying" after 9 instead of 5). The procedure feels arbitrary and brittle.

Connecting to the Student Experience

  • This is the reality for a student who has not mastered multiplicative reasoning. They see the algorithm as a list of steps to mimic.
  • Lacking a deep understanding of place value, the shifting of partial products feels like a magical rule.
  • Without number sense in this system, you have no way to estimate if your answer is reasonable. This is why students will produce answers that are off by orders of magnitude and have no idea.

The Intended Frustration

To find a common denominator, you must use the painful, cognitively-loading multiplication procedure from Exercise 1. The entire process feels slow, insecure, and incredibly frustrating. You are performing a procedure you barely trust to solve a problem you don't intuitively understand.

Connecting to the Student Experience

  • A student who isn't fluent with multiplication facts sees the task of finding a common denominator not as a single step, but as a cascade of difficult, error-prone calculations.
  • This is why students jump to procedural shortcuts like "cross-multiplication" without understanding why it works. It avoids the painful underlying arithmetic.
  • The student who lacks a conceptual model (like a number line) also just pushes symbols around. They don't see that the two fractions have the same value, just that one "turns into" the other.

Exercise 3: The Relational Meaning of "Equals"

The symbol ⇔ means "is the same quantity as." Find the missing value that makes the statement true.

D ⊕ B ⇔ E ⊕

(Where ⊕ means addition)

The Intended Frustration

Your brain, trained for operational math, will scream that the answer to D + B (4+2) is $AZ$ ($10_{six}$). The impulse is to put $AZ$ in the blank. But this is wrong. The statement asks what value, when added to E, creates the same total quantity as D + B. You are being forced to hold the value of one side of the equation in your head while working on the other—to think relationally.

Connecting to the Student Experience

  • A student with an operational view of "=" sees `8 + 4 = __ + 5` and immediately writes `12` in the blank, because they believe the equals sign is a command to compute what comes before it.
  • They are not thinking about balancing the two sides of the equation. This habit makes solving for a variable in algebra conceptually impossible.

Exercise 4: The Capstone - Proportional Reasoning

A recipe requires **B** drops of Dragon's Blood for every **C** grams of Unicorn Horn. You have **AD** drops of Dragon's Blood. How many grams of Unicorn Horn do you need? Set up a proportion and solve for the unknown.

B⁄C ⇔ AD⁄

The Intended Frustration

This task is designed to be overwhelming. To solve it, you must set up the proportion, think relationally, use inverse operations (cross-multiply and divide), and execute all the painful alien arithmetic from the previous exercises. A failure or weakness in any of the previous exercises will cause a total breakdown here. This is the point of compounding failure.

Connecting to the Student Experience

  • This single problem represents the wall that three out of four 8th graders hit. It reveals that "being bad at math" is often not the cause, but the result of specific, unaddressed foundational weaknesses.
  • If **multiplicative reasoning** is weak, the calculations are impossible. If the **concept of a fraction** is just a rule, setting up the ratio is not intuitive. If the **equals sign is operational**, the entire idea of a proportion as a statement of equivalent ratios makes no sense.
  • The student is left stranded, unable to even begin, feeling that math is an arbitrary set of rules that they will never understand. This is the empathy we seek to build.
